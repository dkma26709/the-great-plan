# §4 Attacks and Combat


### Core Principle — Range, Not State

There is no "locked-in" combat state. A model may use a weapon against an enemy model within that weapon's reach, subject to line of sight. What distinguishes melee from ranged is the weapon's reach — a 1" sword and an 8" javelin are the same mechanic at different distances.

The turn is phased (see §7), and attacks of each type resolve in their own phase:

- **Ranged weapons** fire in the Shooting phase
- **Melee weapons** strike in the Combat phase, resolved in Initiative order across both armies

A model is considered *engaged* — for shooting eligibility, pivot restrictions, and related — when any enemy model is within melee reach of either side. Engaged models cannot shoot.

### Weapon Reach

| Category | Reach | Examples |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Close combat weapons | 1" | Hand weapons, clubs, fists, claws, bites |
| Reach weapons | 2" | Spears, halberds, polearms, monster jaws |
| Long reach | 3" | Pikes, lances (on the charge), monster tails |
| Short ranged | 8-12" | Javelins, blowpipes, throwing axes |
| Ranged | 18-36" | Bows, crossbows, giant bow |
| Artillery | 36"+ | War machines, engines |

A model may make attacks with a weapon against any enemy model within that weapon's reach, provided line of sight.

### How Models Attack

In the Shooting or Combat phase, each model in the activating unit makes its attacks against eligible targets (enemies within reach, in arc, with LOS — see §7 for phase-specific eligibility rules). A model may split attacks between different weapons if it has multiple weapon profiles, targeting different models if desired.

### Wound Allocation

When an attacker causes wounds against a defending unit, the defender allocates them among their models — subject to one constraint: **models currently within the attacker's weapon reach must take wounds first.** Only once those in-reach models are either slain or all their wounds allocated may wounds spill onto models out of reach.

For multi-wound models (Kroxigor, heroes, monsters), wounds stack on a single model until that model dies. A single attack's damage does not split across multiple models.

> This rule gives the front rank its role — they take hits first, so they are the ones exposed to enemy strikes. It also reinforces Initiative: faster attackers cut through front-rank defenders before those defenders can strike back, denying them their contribution to the combat.

### Strike Order

In the Combat phase, all units with at least one model in melee reach of an enemy fight. Strikes are resolved by **Initiative**, high to low, across both armies. Ties are simultaneous.

**Models slain before their strike step do not attack.** If a higher-Initiative attacker kills a defender before the defender's I step, the defender contributes nothing further to the combat. This is why Initiative matters so much — it can deny an opponent their attacks entirely.

**Charging bonus:** A unit that charged this turn gains **+1 Initiative** for its first round of attacks. A unit of I4 Skinks charging strikes at effective I5. Specific weapons may further modify this. (See §6 for charge ranges and arcs, §7 for phase structure.)

**Impact attacks** (weapons with the Impact keyword — see §8) resolve **immediately on successful charge contact, within the Charges sub-phase** — before the Combat phase begins. Only triggered on the turn of the charge. Represents the violence of a massive body hitting home.

### When Is It "Combat"?

Combat occurs when models from two or more opposing units are within weapon reach of each other — meaning both sides can attack. This matters for:

- **Combat resolution** (see below) — only applies when both sides can fight
- **Fall back** — only the loser of a mutual combat falls back
- **Stress from losing combat** — only generated by mutual combat, not from being shot at

A unit being peppered by javelins from 8" away is *taking casualties*, but it's not *in combat*. It doesn't test for combat resolution. It might accumulate stress from casualties (25%+ in a phase), but that's a different trigger.

### Combat Resolution

At the end of each combat in the Combat phase — where a *combat* is the group of all opposing units mutually in melee reach, treated as one melee regardless of how many units are present — after all attacks resolve:

1. Each side totals **melee wounds caused** in this turn's Combat phase (after saves). Shooting-phase wounds do not count — they are casualties the unit suffered, not combat contributions.
2. Add modifiers:
   - **Charge bonus:** +1 if you charged this turn
   - **Standard bearer:** +1
   - **Battle standard nearby:** +1
3. Side with higher total wins the combat
4. **Difference** = winner's total minus loser's total

The CR number is computed at the end of the Combat phase. Fall-back resolves immediately after combat resolution, still within the Combat phase. Stress consequences are evaluated at the Start of the next Turn (§7.1). Mid-combat state changes do not disrupt Initiative-ordered strike sequences.

> **Note on flanking:** Flank and rear attacks don't grant a combat resolution modifier. Their effect is to strip the defender of directional Formed benefits (see §6) — a Formed unit hit in the flank loses its charge resistance; hit in the rear it loses all Formed benefits. The natural damage differential (fewer defender models can reach back from awkward angles) still applies geometrically.

### Losing Combat — Fall Back

In the End of Turn phase, for each combat resolved that turn, the loser falls back along the shortest path directly away from the nearest model of the enemy unit that defeated it — bending around friendly units and impassable terrain as needed. The fall-back distance equals the combat difference (minimum 1", maximum 6") and is measured **along the path**, not linearly.

The winning unit then chooses:

- **Follow up:** advance the same distance, staying within weapon reach
- **Hold:** remain in position — the gap opens, both units are now potentially out of weapon reach

Some unit-specific rules may compel one choice or the other (e.g., *Pursuit Predator* forces Follow up).

**Interpenetration is not allowed for fall-backs.** A fleeing unit cannot phase through its own lines — friendly bodies genuinely get in the way.

**If the full fall-back path is blocked** (the unit cannot travel the required distance without violating friendly-unit separation or entering impassable terrain), the unit moves as far as it can along the path, then takes **+1 stress per inch of blocked fall-back**, to a maximum of +3 stress (the normal combat-stress cap).

**If the winning unit wipes out its opponent** (all models slain), the default is to **hold** position — no pursuit. A unit-specific special rule (such as *Pursuit Predator*) may grant or compel a free move toward the nearest enemy instead.

### Disengaging

A unit with models in melee reach of an enemy may move away during Normal Movement (§7.2b). This is not a special action — it's just movement, using the unit's normal allowance.

**A unit in melee reach of any enemy at the start of its activation cannot declare a charge this turn.** It must disengage via normal movement first; redirecting to a new target is a multi-turn commitment.

Fast units disengage easily. Slow units caught by fast enemies struggle to create distance. The "lock" of melee is a natural consequence of relative speed, not a rule.

> **Open question:** Should moving away from an enemy within close combat reach (1") provoke a free strike — one attack per model in range? This would add a cost to disengaging from melee specifically, without preventing it. Worth playtesting both ways.
